# Wisdom, Intuition, Knowledge, and Judgment: A Multidisciplinary Map for Prompt Design

**Version 2.0 — QA-integrated**

---

## Executive Summary

Two claims are under examination:

1. *"Wisdom and intuition are different from knowledge and judgment."*
2. *"Wisdom is the application of acquired knowledge through the lens of personal experience and the use of intuition to guide your actions and strategy."*

Both are **broadly defensible and well-aligned with scholarship** across philosophy, psychology, neuroscience, Eastern contemplative traditions, and decision science. Across these traditions, wisdom and intuition are repeatedly distinguished from propositional knowledge and analytic judgment — but the dominant scholarly view treats these four as *interdependent and overlapping*, not cleanly separable. The strongest analytical prompt will frame them as a system of mutually constituting capacities rather than four bins.

The most important refinements to the user's formulation: it maps closely (though not exactly) onto **Aristotle's phronesis** and the modern **Common Wisdom Model** (Grossmann et al., 2020), but it under-weights two features nearly every contemporary framework treats as central: **moral grounding** (orientation toward a shared good — though this requirement is itself a substantive position, not a universal; see §5.C) and **metacognitive humility** (knowing the limits of one's knowledge and managing uncertainty). It also leaves the reliability of intuition unconditional, when the strongest empirical result in the field (Kahneman & Klein, 2009) establishes that skilled intuition is *conditionally* reliable — dependent on environment and feedback structure.

### Methodological Note

This report organizes its material along an explicit/propositional vs. tacit/holistic axis. This is a useful heuristic, but it imports a Western dual-process frame (System 1 / System 2) that is itself contested within current cognitive science. Bago & De Neys (2019, 2020) blur the boundary with their "competing intuitions" model, and Mega, Gigerenzer & Volz (2015) present neural evidence that contradicts a "clear-cut dichotomy of the processing systems" and supports "rather a unified model." Applied to Buddhist *prajñā* (a soteriological, transconceptual insight) or Daoist *wu-wei*, this axis flattens what those traditions take to be sui generis modes of knowing. The two-families structure is presented here as a Western-derived organizing lens, not a neutral universal.

## 1. Philosophy / Epistemology

### 1.1 Aristotle's Five Intellectual Virtues

The taxonomy in *Nicomachean Ethics*, Book VI, maps almost directly onto the user's four concepts:

**Epistēmē** — scientific knowledge of necessary, universal truths ("that which could not be otherwise"). This is "knowledge" in its paradigmatic propositional form.

**Technē** — craft/art; productive reason aimed at making something (a product external to the activity). Knowing-how in the productive register.

**Phronēsis** — practical wisdom; the intellectual virtue of deliberating well about how to act and live well in contingent, particular situations. The closest match to the user's "wisdom," with important qualifications (see §1.1a–c below).

**Nous** — intuitive intellect; the direct, non-inferential grasp of first principles. The classical philosophical seat of "intuition," though not identical to the modern cognitive-science sense.

**Sophia** — theoretical wisdom; Aristotle defines it as the combination of nous + epistēmē (intuitive grasp of principles plus demonstrative knowledge), oriented toward the highest and most universal objects of contemplation.

**Three contested sub-claims, flagged as live debates:**

**(a) Does phronesis determine good ends, or only means to ends set by moral virtue?** This is one of the most actively disputed questions in Aristotle scholarship. The textual evidence cuts both ways. Aristotle says "we deliberate not about ends but about what contributes to ends" (NE 1112b12), supporting the means-only reading. But he also says phronesis involves "true apprehension of the end" (1142b35) and that deliberating well about living well "requires grasping both the proper means and ends" (1145a4–6). The mainstream reading holds that moral virtue (*aretē*) sets the right ends and phronesis finds the means; a substantial competing line holds phronesis also apprehends and specifies ends in particular situations. **This report presents both readings without taking a side.** Note: Aristotle would call effective skill at bad ends *deinotēs* (cleverness), explicitly **not** phronesis — the "James Bond villain" cannot have phronesis on any reading.

**(b) Does Aristotle rank technē/phronesis below epistēmē/sophia?** Yes. Sophia is "higher and more serious" because it concerns unchanging, universal truths; phronesis and technē concern the contingent ("what can be otherwise"). But Aristotle holds that human flourishing *requires* phronesis, and that phronesis creates the conditions for the pursuit of sophia.

**(c) The phronesis-intuition tension.** The user's definition emphasizes intuition; Aristotle's phronesis essentially involves *bouleusis* (deliberation) — a reasoned, reflective capacity. A strictly orthodox reading would resist calling phronesis "intuitive." However, Aristotle also grants phronesis a *perceptual* grasp of particulars — a kind of nous of the particular (1142a) — which some scholars (notably the Dreyfusian reading) interpret as quasi-intuitive expert perception. This bridging move is real but contested: it downplays the deliberation Aristotle stresses. The mapping between the user's definition and phronesis is therefore **close but partial**, and depends on how much weight one gives the perceptual vs. deliberative elements.

### 1.2 Knowing-That vs. Knowing-How (Ryle)

Gilbert Ryle ("Knowing How and Knowing That," *Proceedings of the Aristotelian Society*, 1945; *The Concept of Mind*, 1949) argued that "knowing how" (practical ability/skill) is not reducible to "knowing that" (propositional/factual knowledge). His regress argument: if every intelligent act required first contemplating a proposition (a rule), then contemplating *that* proposition would itself be an act requiring a prior rule, ad infinitum. Therefore intelligence is exhibited *in* skilled action, not in a prior internal act of theorizing.

This is a cornerstone for distinguishing wisdom/intuition (knowing-how, knowing-how-to-live) from knowledge (knowing-that).

**Live debate:** Jason Stanley and Timothy Williamson ("Knowing How," *Journal of Philosophy* 98: 411–444, 2001) contest Ryle's anti-intellectualism, arguing that knowing-how is in fact a species of propositional knowledge — specifically, knowing, of some way *w*, that *w* is a way to do it. The debate remains active.

### 1.3 Kant: Intuition and Judgment

**Kant's *Anschauung*** (intuition) is a technical term largely unrelated to the colloquial "gut feeling." For Kant, intuition is the immediate, singular mode by which objects are *given* to the mind through sensibility (vs. concepts, by which objects are *thought* through the understanding): "intuitions without concepts are blind, concepts without intuitions are empty." Kant denies humans have *intellectual* intuition (direct access to things-in-themselves); we have only *sensible* intuition structured by space and time. This is "intuition" as the receptive ground of knowledge, not a wisdom faculty.

**Kant's *Urteilskraft*** (power of judgment) is the faculty of subsuming particulars under universals (rules/concepts). He distinguishes *determinative* judgment (the rule is given; apply it to a particular) from *reflective* judgment (only the particular is given; find the universal). Crucially, there can be no rule for applying rules — else a regress of rules-for-applying-rules follows. Kant: "Deficiency in judgement is properly that which is called stupidity, and for such a failing there is no remedy" (*Critique of Pure Reason*, Analytic of Principles). Judgment is a "peculiar talent which can be practised only, and cannot be taught."

**This is the key bridge:** judgment is where knowledge meets the particular case, and it ultimately bottoms out in something trained and quasi-intuitive rather than fully propositional — exactly the gap that phronesis/wisdom fills.

### 1.4 Bergson: Intuition as Metaphysical Method

Henri Bergson (*An Introduction to Metaphysics*, 1903) defines intuition as "the kind of intellectual sympathy by which one places oneself within an object in order to coincide with what is unique in it and consequently inexpressible" — this is Bergson's own phrase in the T. E. Hulme translation. He contrasts this with *analysis* (which only ever circles an object from outside via concepts and symbols). Bergson rehabilitates intuition as a route to absolute knowledge that Kant had foreclosed.

Importantly, for Bergson this is a rigorous *metaphysical method* for grasping *durée* (lived time), not a hunch — a point on which Bergson was widely misread (famously by Russell). This is much closer to the user's sense of intuition as a guiding faculty cultivated through experience.

## 2. Cognitive Science / Psychology

### 2.1 Dual-Process Theory (Kahneman)

Daniel Kahneman's framework (*Thinking, Fast and Slow*, 2011): System 1 is fast, automatic, effortless, associative, intuitive; System 2 is slow, effortful, deliberate, analytic. This maps the intuition/knowledge axis onto cognitive architecture — with a critical warning: System 1 is the source of both expert intuition *and* systematic bias.

**Attribution correction:** The best short characterization of expert intuition — "intuition is nothing more and nothing less than recognition" — is **Herbert Simon's formulation**, quoted approvingly by Kahneman in *Thinking, Fast and Slow*. Simon's full framing: "The situation has provided a cue; this cue has given the expert access to information stored in memory, and the information provides the answer."

**Challenge to the clean dual-process split:** Bago & De Neys (2017, 2019, 2020) challenge the strict "default-interventionist" model (System 2 corrects System 1). Their "smart System 1" / logical-intuition account holds that people often generate a correct response *intuitively* — the mechanism is competition between two intuitions (heuristic and logical) whose relative strength determines the output. This means the boundary between "intuitive" and "analytical" processing is blurrier than the canonical model suggests.

### 2.2 The Kahneman–Klein Détente: When to Trust Intuition

This is the single most important empirical boundary condition for the user's claims.

Kahneman (heuristics-and-biases tradition, skeptical of intuition) and Gary Klein (naturalistic decision-making, champion of expert intuition) co-authored "Conditions for Intuitive Expertise: A Failure to Disagree" (*American Psychologist* 64(6): 515–526, 2009). Their agreed conclusion: **skilled intuitions require (1) a "high-validity" environment (one with stable, learnable regularities) and (2) adequate opportunities for learning — prolonged practice with feedback that is both rapid and unequivocal.** Where these conditions fail (stock picking, long-range political forecasting, clinical judgment without outcome data), confident intuition becomes systematic error.

Critically, **subjective confidence is not a valid cue to accuracy.** Kahneman calls this the "illusion of validity" — a term he coined from his Israeli Army officer-selection experience and elaborated in *Thinking, Fast and Slow* (2011, Ch. 20): "The dismal truth about the quality of our predictions had no effect whatsoever on how we evaluated new candidates and very little effect on the confidence we had in our judgments and predictions."

### 2.3 Klein's Recognition-Primed Decision (RPD) Model

From field studies of firefighters, ER nurses, and military commanders: experts don't compare options. They (1) recognize a situation as a familiar prototype, which primes (2) a single plausible course of action, which they (3) mentally simulate, and act if it passes. Serial pattern-matching plus simulation, not parallel option comparison. This operationalizes "expert intuition" as recognition — and directly supports the user's emphasis on experience as the transforming lens.

### 2.4 The Dreyfus Model of Skill Acquisition

Hubert L. Dreyfus and Stuart E. Dreyfus (*Mind over Machine*, 1986) proposed five stages of skill acquisition — Novice → Advanced Beginner → Competent → Proficient → Expert — with a sixth stage, **Mastery**, added later (formally restated in Rousse & Dreyfus, 2021). The arc is precisely a movement *from* explicit rule-following *toward* intuitive, holistic, non-deliberative response. Novices follow context-free rules slowly and consciously; experts perceive what a situation calls for directly and act fluidly without decomposing it into rules. This directly supports the "lens of personal experience" claim.

### 2.5 The Psychology of Wisdom: Three Flagship Models

**Berlin Wisdom Paradigm (Baltes & Staudinger, 2000).** Baltes & Staudinger (*American Psychologist* 55(1): 122–136) define wisdom as "an expert knowledge system concerning the fundamental pragmatics of life that permits exceptional insight, judgment, and advice about complex and uncertain matters." Assessed via think-aloud responses to ill-defined life dilemmas, scored on five criteria: (1) rich factual knowledge about life, (2) rich procedural knowledge (strategies), (3) lifespan contextualism, (4) relativism of values and life priorities, (5) recognition and management of uncertainty. This is a strongly cognitive/knowledge-based model. Ardelt critiqued it precisely for treating wisdom as a body of knowledge that could exist "independently of its manifestation within people" (the Berlin group's own explicit framing) and for omitting the personal/affective dimension.

**Ardelt's Three-Dimensional Wisdom Model (3D-WM, 2003).** Wisdom = integration of three dimensions: *cognitive* (deep understanding of life, including its limits and uncertainties), *reflective* (self-examination, perspective-taking, absence of projection/blame), and *affective/compassionate* (empathy, care for others). Measured via a 39-item self-report scale (14 cognitive, 12 reflective, 13 affective items). Published validity work shows positive associations with well-being, purpose in life, and self-mastery, and negative associations with depression and death anxiety — though individual correlate verification against the primary tables (Ardelt 2003, *Research on Aging* 25(3): 275–324) is recommended for publication-critical use.

**Grossmann's Common Wisdom Model (2020).** The current center of gravity in wisdom science. Grossmann, Weststrate, Ardelt, Brienza, Dong, Ferrari, Fournier, Hu, Nusbaum & Vervaeke (*Psychological Inquiry* 31(2), "The Science of Wisdom in a Polarized World"), p. 108, define wisdom as **"morally grounded excellence in certain aspects of meta-cognition"** — specifically **perspectival metacognition**: recognition of the limits of knowledge, awareness of context and change, perspective-taking, and integration of different viewpoints/interests. The "moral grounding" component involves prosocial orientation toward shared humanity, avoidance of excessive self-interest, and the pursuit of truth. Grossmann's empirical signature finding: wise reasoning is *situation-dependent* (varies within persons across contexts) and can be boosted by **self-distancing** (ego-decentering). Glück & Weststrate (2022, "The Wisdom Researchers and the Elephant") argue the Common Model captures wise *reasoning* but under-weights *emotional* components needed for wise *behavior*.

**Note on the moral-grounding requirement (see §5.C):** The Common Model deliberately builds in moral grounding, but this is a *substantive position*, not a consensus universal. There are robust morally-neutral conceptions of strategic competence — Aristotle's own *deinotēs* (cleverness), Machiavellian prudence, strategic wisdom in realist IR theory — that look like "wisdom" in the user's sense but deliberately omit moral orientation. A definition lacking moral grounding is not self-evidently deficient; it may track *deinotēs* / strategic prudence rather than full Aristotelian/Grossmannian wisdom.

**Grossmann (2025).** A paper in *Journal of Experimental Psychology: General* ("Wisdom reconsidered: A dynamic network account of metacognition and complex thought") argues perspectival metacognition *integrates* deliberate reflective thought and automatic emotion-driven responses — a direct refinement of any simple System 1 / System 2 split.

**Wisdom and AI.** Johnson, Karimi, Bengio, Chater, Gerstenberg, Larson, Levine, Mitchell, Rahwan, Schölkopf & Grossmann, "Imagining and building wise machines: the centrality of AI metacognition," *Trends in Cognitive Sciences* (Cell Press, 2026; DOI: 10.1016/j.tics.2026.01.002 series) argue wisdom = object-level strategies + metacognitive strategies (intellectual humility, perspective-taking, context-adaptability), and that wisdom is context-sensitive and about *reasoning process*, not just outcomes.

### 2.6 Gigerenzer: Ecological Rationality and Gut Feelings

Gerd Gigerenzer (building on Herbert Simon's bounded rationality/satisficing and Egon Brunswik's lens model) argues that fast-and-frugal heuristics — simple rules like "take-the-best," 1/N, the recognition heuristic — are not defective shortcuts but can *outperform* complex optimization, especially under uncertainty with limited data. The key concept is **ecological rationality**: a heuristic is "rational" when matched to the structure of its environment.

**Attribution note:** "Ecological rationality" as a modern research program has two independent founders — Gigerenzer (psychology) and Vernon L. Smith (experimental economics; 2002 Nobel), with deeper roots in Simon and Brunswik. Smith's sense emphasizes emergent institutional/cultural rationality; Gigerenzer's emphasizes individual-level heuristic-environment fit. Both lineages matter for a full picture.

Gigerenzer's reframing rehabilitates intuition as *intelligent* — not irrational — providing a counterweight to the biases-and-heuristics literature: gut feelings are "developed intuitions," unconscious applications of heuristics that exploit evolved capacities.

## 3. Neuroscience

### Prefatory Note

The neural evidence here is largely correlational (fMRI), lesion-based, or patient-study-derived, not causal-manipulation-based. Neuroscience does not support a clean "two brain regions" split, but it consistently shows that intuitive/experiential processing recruits a distributed, partly subcortical, affect- and body-linked network (basal ganglia, vmPFC/OFC, insula, amygdala, dACC) partly distinct from the lateral-prefrontal/parietal network of effortful deliberation — a neural echo of the wisdom/intuition vs. knowledge/judgment distinction. Treat localization claims as suggestive, not settled.

### 3.1 Damasio's Somatic Marker Hypothesis (SMH)

Decisions under uncertainty are biased by "somatic markers" — bodily/emotional signals (gut feelings, skin-conductance responses) that tag options with affective value based on prior experience. These are processed especially in the **ventromedial prefrontal cortex (vmPFC)** and amygdala. The classic empirical anchor is the Iowa Gambling Task (Bechara, Damasio, Tranel & Damasio, "Deciding Advantageously Before Knowing the Advantageous Strategy," *Science* 275(5304): 1293–1295, 1997): "Normals began to choose advantageously before they realized which strategy worked best, whereas prefrontal patients continued to choose disadvantageously even after they knew the correct strategy." Healthy participants developed anticipatory skin-conductance responses to risky decks before they could consciously articulate which were bad; vmPFC-lesion patients never developed these signals.

This gives intuition/wisdom a neurobiological substrate: experience is stored as embodied value signals guiding action pre-consciously.

**Critical review:** Dunn, Dalgleish & Lawrence ("The somatic marker hypothesis: A critical evaluation," *Neuroscience & Biobehavioral Reviews* 30(2): 239–271, **2006**) conclude the SMH is "underspecified" and lacks decisive empirical support — compounded by Maia & McClelland's challenge that Iowa Gambling Task participants have more explicit knowledge than the SMH assumes. The SMH is influential but not settled.

### 3.2 Lieberman's X-System vs. C-System

Matthew Lieberman ("Intuition: A Social Cognitive Neuroscience Approach," *Psychological Bulletin* 126(1): 109–137, 2000; *Annual Review of Psychology* 58: 259–289, 2007) maps dual-process cognition onto two neural systems. The **X-system** (reflexive/automatic/intuitive): amygdala, basal ganglia, vmPFC, lateral temporal cortex, dorsal anterior cingulate cortex. The **C-system** (reflective/controlled/deliberative): lateral and medial PFC, lateral/medial parietal cortex, medial temporal lobe (hippocampus), rostral ACC. Lieberman's signature claim: "the caudate and putamen, in the basal ganglia, are central components of both intuition and implicit learning." These are hypothesized correlates synthesized from imaging and lesion data (e.g., Parkinson's/Huntington's studies), not single causal experiments.

### 3.3 Volz & von Cramon: The Orbitofrontal "Gist Detector"

Kirsten Volz and D. Yves von Cramon ("What neuroscience can tell about intuitive processes in the context of perceptual discovery," *Journal of Cognitive Neuroscience* 18(12): 2077–2087, 2006) studied intuitive "coherence judgments" (sensing a fragmented image is meaningful before identifying it). The **orbitofrontal cortex / vmPFC** acts as a fast "gist" detector — left-OFC activation scaled with the probability of a preliminary perception of coherence, experienced subjectively as a hunch *before* explicit recognition. The **anterior cingulate cortex** is implicated in monitoring conflict/uncertainty. A later MEG study (Horr, Braun & Volz, 2014, *Cognitive, Affective, & Behavioral Neuroscience*) refined the OFC timing.

### 3.4 The Insula and Interoception (Craig)

A. D. "Bud" Craig (*Nature Reviews Neuroscience* 10: 59–70, 2009, "How do you feel — now? The anterior insula and human awareness"; see also Craig, *Nature Reviews Neuroscience* 3: 655–666, 2002) showed the **posterior insula** receives primary interoceptive signals (the physiological state of the body) and the **anterior insular cortex (AIC)** re-represents and integrates them into conscious subjective feelings. Craig explicitly links the AIC to "decision making and sudden insight." This is the neural machinery by which bodily experience becomes the "felt sense" of intuition — though the insula→intuition link rests heavily on somatic-marker theory and is more inferential than the procedural-memory story. Craig's 2009 AIC awareness framing is explicitly a Perspective article, not settled consensus.

### 3.5 Basal Ganglia and Skill-Based Intuition (Graybiel)

Ann Graybiel ("The basal ganglia and chunking of action repertoires," *Neurobiology of Learning and Memory* 70(1–2): 119–136, 1998; Graybiel & Grafton, "The Striatum: Where Skills and Habits Meet," *Cold Spring Harbor Perspectives in Biology* 7(8): a021691, 2015) showed the **striatum chunks motor and cognitive action sequences** into automatic "performance units" acquired slowly and without conscious awareness. These mechanisms "generalize to other domains, including cognitive skills and emotion-related patterns of action" (2015, abstract). There is an anterior→posterior striatal shift as sequences become well-learned (effortful → automatic). This is the most mechanistically robust neural account of how Dreyfus-style expertise/intuition is built.

## 4. Eastern Philosophy and Contemplative Traditions

### 4.1 Buddhist Prajñā vs. Vijñāna

*Vijñāna* (Pali: viññāṇa) is "discriminative consciousness" — the Sanskrit prefix *vi-* connotes discrimination/division/separation. It is ordinary object-focused knowing (the six sense-consciousnesses), knowing "about" things. *Prajñā* (Pali: paññā) — the prefix *pra-* connotes intensification and forward/before — is wisdom/insight that goes *beyond* mere knowing-about to apprehend the true nature of phenomena (impermanence, non-self, emptiness). Translators note prajñā is closer to "insight," "discriminating knowledge," or "intuitive apprehension" than to "knowledge" (see Damien Keown, *A Dictionary of Buddhism*, Oxford). A near-exact Eastern parallel to the wisdom/knowledge distinction: prajñā ≈ wisdom/intuitive insight; vijñāna ≈ discriminative knowledge/judgment. Crucially, prajñā must be *experientially realized*, not merely conceptually understood — directly supporting the "lens of personal experience" claim.

**Caveat:** Prajñā is embedded in a soteriological framework (the path to liberation/nirvāṇa) that has no direct equivalent in Aristotle or cognitive science. The parallel is a family resemblance, not an identity.

### 4.2 Daoist Wu-Wei and Cook Ding

"Effortless action" or "non-forcing" — acting in spontaneous accord with the natural order (Dao). The paradigm is Zhuangzi's **Cook Ding**, who has **kept the same blade keen for nineteen years** while cutting thousands of oxen, because he "follows the natural lines," moving through the spaces in the joints rather than hacking. Cook Ding says what he cares about is "the Way [Dao], which goes beyond mere skill" (Burton Watson translation) — explicitly elevating intuitive, attuned mastery above technical skill (technē). This is intuition-as-cultivated-spontaneity: skilled, non-deliberative, responsive action that emerges only after long experience.

**Scholarly counterpoint:** Eric Schwitzgebel ("The Unskilled Zhuangzi") and others argue the *Inner Chapters* do not straightforwardly celebrate skill mastery — skill is criticized at least as often as praised, and the Cook Ding passage can be read as much about the knife's passivity / nourishing life as about expertise. Using Cook Ding as a clean emblem of "skilled non-deliberative mastery" is one reading, not the only one.

### 4.3 Cross-Traditional Equivalence: A Family Resemblance, Not an Identity

The report's organizing observation — that phronesis, prajñā, and wu-wei converge on "non-propositional, experience-cultivated, value-laden knowing-in-action" — is real and striking but must be held as a **loose family resemblance**, not an equivalence.

The three concepts have radically different *aims*: phronesis targets *eudaimonia* and right action in the polis (and, on orthodox reading, is deliberative); prajñā targets liberation through insight into emptiness/no-self; wu-wei targets alignment with the Dao. Comparative philosophers caution against collapsing them. They share a structural property — non-propositional, experience-cultivated, value-laden action — but differ in metaphysics, soteriology, and practical orientation. The convergence is presented here as resonance, not identity.

### 4.4 Indigenous Knowledge Systems

Indigenous and oral traditions characteristically transmit wisdom through lived experience, story, apprenticeship, ceremony, and relationship to land/community rather than through decontextualized propositional statements — knowledge as relational, place-based, and embodied, integrating the practical, ethical, and spiritual. This resonates strongly with the tacit/phronetic conception of wisdom. **This section is under-sourced in the current research pass.** A dedicated inquiry engaging named scholars and specific frameworks (e.g., Two-Eyed Seeing / Etuaptmumk from Mi'kmaw Elder Albert Marshall; Robin Wall Kimmerer's work on Traditional Ecological Knowledge; Linda Tuhiwai Smith on decolonizing methodologies) is recommended before relying on this as more than a general claim.

## 5. Decision Science / Strategy

### 5.1 Clausewitz's Coup d'Oeil

In *On War* (Book I, Chapter 3, "The Genius for War"; Graham 1873 translation), the military genius possesses *coup d'œil* ("stroke of the eye") — the intuitive capacity to grasp the truth of a situation at a glance. Clausewitz describes this truth-discovery faculty and then says it is "figuratively expressed by the French phrase *coup d'œil*": "the rapid discovery of a truth which to the ordinary mind is either not visible at all or only becomes so after long examination and reflection." This is paired with *determination* (the courage to act on that inner light) and explicitly grounded in absorbed experience ("examples from history"). Intuition operationalized for strategy under fog and uncertainty.

**Translation note:** Wording differs between the Graham (1873) and Howard/Paret (1976) editions; the quote above follows Graham.

### 5.2 Boyd's OODA Loop

John Boyd's Observe–Orient–Decide–Act cycle. The crucial, often-omitted feature: **Orientation is the "big O"** (shaped by experience, culture, prior knowledge), and in Boyd's final diagram ("The Essence of Winning and Losing," 28 June 1995) there are direct **"Implicit Guidance and Control"** links running from Orientation to Observe and Act — *bypassing* explicit Decision when one has deep, shared orientation. Boyd linked this to *Fingerspitzengefühl* ("fingertip feeling," intuitive skill). Tempo advantage comes from collapsing deliberation into trained intuition — a direct strategic instantiation of the user's thesis.

**Source caveats:** Boyd never published in the conventional academic sense. He produced briefings ("A Discourse on Winning and Losing," "Patterns of Conflict") and one short essay ("Destruction and Creation," 1976); his collected work was published posthumously (Air University Press, ed. Grant Hammond, 2018). The widely quoted line — "This stuff has got to be implicit; if it is explicit, you can't do it fast enough" — **cannot be traced to a primary written source** and appears to derive from those who attended his briefings (Coram, Richards). Treat as orally transmitted / secondhand, not a verified written quotation.

### 5.3 Polanyi's Tacit Knowledge

Michael Polanyi (*The Tacit Dimension*, 1966, p. 4): "I shall reconsider human knowledge by starting from the fact that we can know more than we can tell." All knowledge has a tacit, personal, inarticulable root (we recognize a face without being able to specify how). For Polanyi, declarative knowledge may be needed to *acquire* a skill but becomes unnecessary once one is expert; tacit knowing underlies all expertise and even scientific discovery. David Autor named "Polanyi's Paradox" in 2014 (NBER Working Paper No. 20485, prepared for the Federal Reserve Bank of Kansas City's Jackson Hole symposium) — the reason many human capacities resisted automation.

**Misinterpretation flag:** Nonaka & Takeuchi's popular "SECI model" (tacit→explicit knowledge conversion) is widely regarded by Polanyi scholars as a misinterpretation. Polanyi held tacit knowing to be *irreducible* — it can be cultivated and indwelt but not fully converted to explicit propositions. This critique is made by Stephen Gourlay (2006), Harry Collins, Dave Snowden (2003), and others, on the grounds that SECI's "externalization" step assumes precisely the conversion Polanyi denied.

### 5.4 Weick's Sensemaking

Karl Weick reframed organizations away from rational-choice decision-making toward **sensemaking**: "the ongoing retrospective development of plausible images that rationalize what people are doing" (Weick, Sutcliffe & Obstfeld, "Organizing and the Process of Sensemaking," *Organization Science* 16(4): 409–421, 2005, p. 409). Seven properties: grounded in identity, retrospective, enactive (we partly create the environments we then interpret), social, ongoing, focused on extracted cues, and driven by plausibility rather than accuracy.

His recipe is captured by a line he borrows (not originates): "How can I know what I think until I see what I say?" — traceable to Graham Wallas (*The Art of Thought*, 1926) and popularized by E. M. Forster (*Aspects of the Novel*, 1927).

Sensemaking is wisdom-adjacent: it is how actors construct meaning and act under ambiguity where full knowledge is impossible — privileging plausible, action-enabling interpretation over complete information. Weick's Mann Gulch and Tenerife case studies document sensemaking's *failures* as well as its logic.

## 6. How the User's Formulation Compares to Existing Frameworks

The user's claim — *"Wisdom is the application of acquired knowledge through the lens of personal experience and the use of intuition to guide your actions and strategy"* — is a **practical, phronesis-adjacent, action-oriented definition**. Its fit with the scholarship:

### Strengths

- It correctly distinguishes wisdom from inert knowledge (echoing Aristotle, Ryle, Polanyi).
- It correctly centers **experience** as the transforming lens (Dreyfus, Klein's RPD, the Berlin model's lifespan contextualism, Damasio's stored somatic markers, Buddhist experiential realization of prajñā).
- It correctly includes **intuition** as an action-guiding component (Simon/Klein, Gigerenzer, Boyd, wu-wei).
- It is explicitly **action/strategy-oriented**, aligning with phronesis, RPD, OODA, and sensemaking.

### Gaps Relative to Scholarly Consensus

**1. No moral/ethical dimension.** Phronesis, Ardelt's compassion dimension, and the Common Wisdom Model's "moral grounding" all treat orientation toward a good (one's own and others' flourishing) as *constitutive* of wisdom. By this consensus, a brilliant, experienced, intuitive strategist serving purely selfish or destructive ends would be *clever* or *expert*, not *wise*. **However** (see §5.C stress-test above): this moral-grounding requirement is a substantive position, not a universal. The user's definition might be tracking strategic prudence / *deinotēs* rather than full moral-philosophical wisdom — and that is a legitimate concept in its own right. The prompt should surface and examine this choice, not assume it.

**2. No metacognitive humility / uncertainty management.** Nearly every model (Berlin's "management of uncertainty," Grossmann's intellectual humility, Socratic "knowing that you do not know") treats *awareness of the limits of one's knowledge* as central. The user's definition is confident and applicative; wisdom science says wisdom is also reflective and self-limiting.

**3. No boundary conditions on intuition.** The Kahneman–Klein (2009) result means intuition should be trusted *conditionally* — in high-validity domains with rapid, unequivocal feedback. A complete formulation would specify *when* intuition guides well vs. when it misleads. The "illusion of validity" demonstrates that subjective confidence is not a reliable indicator of intuitive accuracy.

**4. "Knowledge" and "judgment" are under-theorized in the claim.** The prompt should distinguish knowing-that (epistēmē) from knowing-how (technē/skill), and treat judgment (Kant's Urteilskraft / the deliberative element of phronesis) as the *bridge* faculty that applies knowledge to particulars — the place where wisdom and intuition actually do their work.

---

## 7. Recommendations for Prompt Design

**Stage 1 — Anchor on a two-axis structure, not four silos.**

Define the axis explicitly: explicit/propositional/articulable/rule-based (knowledge, analytic judgment) vs. tacit/holistic/experiential/action-embedded (wisdom, intuition). Then *immediately* require the analysis to show how the four interpenetrate: knowledge feeds intuition via chunking (Graybiel); judgment applies knowledge but bottoms out in trained perception (Kant); wisdom integrates all three under moral and metacognitive governance (Grossmann). **Flag that this axis is itself a heuristic lens** — a Western dual-process frame that is contested within cognitive science and imperfectly translatable to Eastern and Indigenous frameworks.

**Stage 2 — Force each concept to be defined against its nearest neighbor.**

Use the canonical contrasts: wisdom vs. knowledge (phronesis vs. epistēmē; prajñā vs. vijñāna); intuition vs. analysis (System 1 vs. System 2; Bergson vs. Kant; coup d'œil vs. staff planning); judgment vs. wisdom (applying a rule to a case vs. discerning what good end to pursue — noting this framing reflects one reading of phronesis); knowing-how vs. knowing-that (Ryle — noting the Stanley/Williamson counter).

**Stage 3 — Incorporate the three additions the user's definition omits.**

(a) Moral grounding / orientation toward a good — while noting this is a substantive commitment, not a universal, and that the user may be tracking *deinotēs*/strategic prudence. (b) Metacognitive humility and uncertainty management. (c) The conditional reliability of intuition (Kahneman–Klein: high-validity environments + rapid feedback). These convert the definition from "expert decisiveness" into "wisdom" as the contemporary scholarly consensus understands it.

**Stage 4 — Demand multidisciplinary triangulation by design.**

Require any claim about these concepts to be tested against at least three lenses — a philosophical (Aristotle/Kant/Ryle), an empirical-psychological (Kahneman–Klein, Grossmann, Ardelt), and a cross-cultural/contemplative (prajñā, wu-wei) — to avoid a Western-analytic monoculture. Note the limits of each triangulation: cross-traditional equivalences are family resemblances; neural evidence is correlational; and measurement of wisdom is disputed across the Berlin (performance), Ardelt (self-report), and Grossmann (situated/state) approaches.

**Stage 5 — Build in the failure modes.**

A genuinely analytical (not definitional) prompt should ask *when each mode fails*: intuition in low-validity domains (Kahneman's "illusion of validity"); knowledge without judgment (the person who knows all the rules but can't act — Aristotle's worry, Kant's "stupidity"); sensemaking breakdowns under stress (Weick's Mann Gulch); wisdom's situation-dependence (Grossmann: wise reasoning varies within persons across contexts); Cook Ding's passivity (Schwitzgebel: the passage may not straightforwardly celebrate mastery).

## 8. Consolidated Caveats

**On the four-way separation.** Most scholarship treats these concepts as overlapping and interdependent. Modern strategic-cognition research (Boyd; Duggan on Clausewitz) explicitly argues analysis and intuition are *intertwined*, not separable; Grossmann's 2025 work argues metacognition *integrates* deliberate and automatic processing; Mega, Gigerenzer & Volz (2015) present neural evidence for a "unified model" over a "clear-cut dichotomy." Present the distinction as a useful map, not a literal partition of the mind.

**On the polysemy of "intuition."** It means at least four different things across the sources: Kant's sensibility (*Anschauung*), Aristotle's nous (grasp of first principles), Bergson's sympathetic metaphysical method, and the cognitive-science "expert pattern-recognition / gut feeling" (Simon/Klein/Gigerenzer). The prompt must disambiguate which sense is in play; conflating them produces confusion.

**On neural evidence.** Largely correlational or lesion-based, not causal. The vmPFC/insula/basal-ganglia "intuition network" rests on fMRI correlations and patient studies. The somatic marker hypothesis is influential but critiqued (Dunn et al. 2006). Lieberman's X/C-system region assignments are hypothesized syntheses. Craig's anterior-insula awareness claim is a Perspective. The striatum/chunking account of skill-based intuition (Graybiel) is the most mechanistically robust.

**On wisdom measurement.** The Berlin (performance/expertise), Ardelt (self-report 3D-WS), and Grossmann (situated/state) approaches disagree on whether wisdom is general or personal, trait or state, cognitive or affective — and some scales have limited cross-cultural replication. There is no single validated "wisdom-meter."

**On cross-tradition equivalences.** Phronesis, prajñā, and wu-wei arise from different metaphysics and soteriologies. Their convergence on "non-propositional, experience-cultivated, value-laden knowing-in-action" is real and striking but is a resonance, not an identity.

**On Indigenous knowledge systems.** This section is under-sourced. The relevant claim is presented at a general level. Dedicated inquiry with named scholars and specific traditions (Two-Eyed Seeing, Kimmerer, Smith) is recommended before relying on it.

**On source quality.** Some retrieved sources are secondary/tertiary (encyclopedias, blogs). Primary-source quotations (Aristotle, Kant, Ryle, Polanyi, Bechara et al., Kahneman & Klein, Grossmann et al.) have been verified against the named publications where possible. Translation-dependent quotes (Clausewitz, Zhuangzi, Bergson) vary by edition; the editions used are specified where known.
